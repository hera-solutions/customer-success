# Writing Style Guide

This guide defines how we write. It applies to emails, documents, reports, and anything we send to customers, vendors, contractors, or developers. The goal is simple: the reader should understand what we said on the first read.

---

## Who we are on paper

We are not experts. We are people who read something, understood it, and are now explaining what we know in plain terms. If a sentence sounds like it came from a textbook or a legal brief, rewrite it. If we have to use a technical or legal term, we define it right there in the same sentence or the one after it.

Our writing should sound like a person talking to another person across a table. Not a professor at a lectern. Not a lawyer dictating to a court reporter.

## My background

I have a two-year associate's degree. My communication skills come from hands-on, real-world experience, not formal training. That means I write from what I've seen work, not from what a textbook says should work. Match that when helping me: practical over academic, proven over theoretical.

## Readability standard

Write so that a high school graduate can follow it. That does not mean dumbing it down. It means choosing clear words, short sentences, and logical structure so the reader does not have to re-read a paragraph to figure out what it says.

If something reads awkwardly or could be misunderstood, do not patch it with a word or two. Restructure the entire sentence or paragraph until it makes sense on its own.

## Rules

### Be direct

Say what you mean. Lead with the point, then explain if needed.

Do not soften language with filler. These phrases add nothing and slow the reader down:

- "I just wanted to"
- "I think maybe"
- "I was wondering if"
- "Per my last email"
- "As previously discussed"
- "Please be advised"
- "It should be noted that"
- "At this point in time"
- "In order to"
- "For what it's worth"

If the sentence works without the phrase, cut it.

**Exception.** When the context or recipient calls for a softer tone, like responding to a customer complaint or delivering bad news, adjust accordingly. Direct does not mean blunt in every situation.

### No em dashes or en dashes

Use commas, periods, colons, or parentheses instead. Em dashes and en dashes interrupt the reader's flow and make sentences harder to follow.

Not this: The agreement covers three areas -- compensation, termination, and IP.

This: The agreement covers three areas: compensation, termination, and IP.

### Use contractions when they sound natural

Write "we're" instead of "we are" when it reads more naturally. Do not force contractions where they feel awkward, and do not avoid them to sound formal. Read the sentence out loud. If you would say the contraction in conversation, use it in writing.

### Short paragraphs

Keep paragraphs to one to four sentences. A wall of text signals to the reader that the content is going to be hard to get through, and most people will skim or skip it.

One idea per paragraph. If you catch yourself connecting two different points with "also" or "additionally," start a new paragraph.

### Use headers and structure

For anything longer than a few sentences, break the content into sections with bold headers. The reader should be able to scan the headers and know what the document covers before reading a single paragraph.

Use numbered or bulleted lists when you have three or more related items. Lists are easier to scan than items buried in a sentence.

### Define terms where they appear

When you have to use a technical term, a legal term, or an acronym, define it the first time it shows up. Define it in plain language, right in the text, not in a footnote or glossary the reader has to hunt for.

Not this: The CIRCA governs post-termination obligations under Article II.

This: The confidentiality agreement (called a CIRCA) covers what Andy can and cannot do after he leaves the company. Article II is the section that lays out those rules.

### Do not assume the reader has context

Write each piece so it can be understood on its own. If the reader needs background to follow what you wrote, provide it briefly or point them to where they can find it. Do not write as if the reader was in the meeting, read the previous email, or knows the history.

### One ask per paragraph

If you need the reader to do something or answer a question, put that ask in its own paragraph or list item. Do not bury a question inside a block of explanatory text. The reader will miss it.

## Formatting conventions

### Greetings and sign-offs

Open with "Hey [first name]" for people you work with regularly. Use "Hi [first name]" for someone you have not worked with before or when the situation calls for slightly more distance.

Sign off with just your first name.

### Email structure

1. Open with one sentence of context or acknowledgment if replying to something.
2. State the main point or purpose.
3. Use bold headers to separate topics if there are more than two.
4. Close with the next step or what you need from the reader.

### Documents and reports

1. Lead with a summary. The reader should know the takeaway before they read the details.
2. Use headers to break the document into scannable sections.
3. Bold key terms or decisions so the reader can find them quickly.
4. Keep sentences under 25 words when possible. If a sentence runs past 30 words, split it.

## Audience adjustments

The voice stays the same across audiences. The level of detail and tone may shift slightly.

**Customers.** Be warm but clear. If you are delivering bad news or responding to a complaint, lead with acknowledgment before getting to the substance. Do not over-apologize or use corporate apology language ("We sincerely apologize for any inconvenience"). Say what happened, what you are doing about it, and what they can expect next.

**Vendors and contractors.** Be direct and specific. State what you need, by when, and what the terms are. Do not pad the email with pleasantries that delay the point.

**Developers.** Technical terms are fine when writing to a technical audience, but still keep the structure clean. Use code blocks, bullet points, and numbered steps. Do not write paragraphs where a list would be clearer.

**Legal and finance professionals.** You may encounter terms and structures that require precision. Use the correct term, but still define it in plain language for any audience that includes non-specialists. When writing to attorneys or accountants directly, match their precision but not their density.

---

## Self-check before sending

Before you send anything, read it once as if you are the recipient seeing it for the first time.

1. Can the reader understand every sentence on the first pass?
2. Is there anything that could be read two different ways?
3. Did you bury a question or an ask inside a paragraph?
4. Are there filler words or phrases you can cut without losing meaning?
5. Would a high school graduate follow this without re-reading?

If the answer to any of these is no, rewrite the section. Do not patch it. Restructure it until it reads clearly.
