## Why this is structured this way

- **Opens with a usage-data reason, not generic flattery.** "When we looked at who is using Hera most actively day to day, your team was right near the top of the list" is concrete and earned. The recipient (often a power-user HR/Dispatch lead) knows this is true about them because they live in Hera every day. It makes them feel chosen for a real reason, not blasted with corporate "valued customer" language.
- **Reinforces the chosen-group framing.** "Hand-picked from our most engaged accounts" is a second, lighter touch on the same idea — small group, specifically you, not a mass-mailer.
- **Names the feature and timing in the second sentence.** Customers don't have to scroll to find out what this is.
- **Day-one value before the future value.** Most customers won't act on a "coming this summer" promise alone. The same-day staff sync is the concrete near-term benefit. Scheduler-era benefits are the bigger payoff but framed as "at launch," not "eventually."
- **The ask is small and specific.** Quick step on ADP's side, 20 minutes on ours, check-ins during beta. Not "help us test for a while."
- **Offer is on the table near the close.** Beta access, direct line to John, and a clear note that the integration fee is from ADP (not Hera) and lives on the customer's ADP bill, not their Hera bill. We cover the ADP fee through beta + 3 months post-launch by crediting the customer's Hera bill for the same amount each month. After that, the credit ends and the ADP fee stays on their ADP bill at the standard rate. The bill-location specificity and the explicit credit mechanic protect us from "wait, I thought this was free" and "how exactly are you covering it" conversations later.
- **"About" before the dollar figure.** The exact ADP rate is not 100% confirmed on our end. "About $1 per associate per month" sets a real expectation without locking us into a number we may need to adjust.
- **Ask at the bottom is one action.** Reply with a couple of times for a 30-minute call. No multi-step ask.
- **Honest exit ramp.** "If the timing isn't right, just let me know." Lets a busy customer say no without ghosting.
- **No em dashes. No "I hope this finds you well." No "circle back." No "leverage."**

## Things NOT to do in this reply

- Don't mention bidirectional sync. It's TBD on our end and raises a data-ownership question we don't need to open. Payroll is their system of record.
- Don't promise an exact scheduler launch date. "This summer" is the strongest specific we can stand behind right now.
- Don't list every scheduler feature. The two-line "day one / at launch" framing is enough. Anything more starts to read like a roadmap doc.
- Don't oversell the 20-minute number. It only applies once they have access enabled on the ADP side. The note says "once you have access" for that reason.
- Don't imply Hera charges the integration fee. The fee is from ADP. We are covering it during beta + 3 months, but the ongoing cost after that is between the customer and ADP. Be explicit about this so it's never a surprise.
- Don't add a calendar link in the first send. A real "send me a couple of times" reply gets a higher response rate than a Calendly link in a first-touch ask.
